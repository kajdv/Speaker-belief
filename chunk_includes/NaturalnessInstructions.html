Before we begin, you'll need to familiarize yourself with two different kinds of naturalness.


Consider the following sentence:


    John isn't happy about the rumor that Natalie married the president of Sweden.


This sentence might seem weird for all sorts of reasons. For instance, Sweden doesn't have a president. Who are John and Natalie? And what is this rumor we're talking about, and why would John not be happy about it? Moreover, it seems like quite a strange thing for someone just to say!


However, it's actually a perfectly well-formed sentence of English. If we imagine a better context, we can see that it actually sounds perfectly natural.


    Suppose that that we live in a fictional or future world where Sweden is a republic.

    Natalie is a famous Swedish pop-singer, known for her many scandalous affairs.

    John is the president's advisor and PR-manager.


    John isn't happy about the rumor that Natalie married the president of Sweden.


Much better, right? In this experiment, this sentence would be given a high score, close to Completely natural.


Now compare this to the following, slightly modified version of the sentence, with the same context as above:


    Suppose that that we live in a fictional or future world where Sweden is a republic.

    Natalie is a famous Swedish pop-singer, known for her many scandalous affairs.

    John is the president's advisor and PR-manager.


    John isn't doubting about the rumor that Natalie married the president of Sweden.


No matter how we might alter the context, this sentence is just not a well-formed, or natural sentence of English. This sentence would receive a low score, close to Completely unnatural.
